## Background
- Vulvar cancer is rare (~5% of gynecologic cancers), most commonly in elderly women.
- Most cases are squamous cell carcinoma.
- Brachytherapy is most commonly used as adjuvant/boost after surgery or for recurrence.

## Clinical Indications
1. **Adjuvant boost** after EBRT for high-risk features (positive margins, lymph node involvement, lymphovascular space invasion).
2. **Primary treatment** for small, well-defined T1-T2 tumors unsuitable for surgery.
3. **Re-irradiation** for local recurrence (with caution; BT can achieve 50–70% LC).
4. **Palliation** of symptomatic, locally advanced tumors.

## Applicator Selection
| Lesion | Applicator |
|---|---|
| Vulvar (superficial < 5 mm) | Surface mold / Freiburg flap |
| Vulvar (deeper, ≤ 3 cm) | Interstitial (perineal template, plastic tubes) |
| Recurrent, post-surgery | Interstitial (MUPIT, Syed) or freehand needles |
| Distal urethra / clitoris | Interstitial with surface dose constraints |

## Dose Schedules
- **Adjuvant (post-EBRT, e.g., 45–50 Gy):** 3 × 4–5 Gy to HR-CTV D90; cumulative EQD2 ≈ 65–75 Gy.
- **Primary (definitive):** 5–6 × 4.5–5.5 Gy to HR-CTV D90; cumulative EQD2 ≈ 70–80 Gy.
- **Re-irradiation (with prior EBRT):** 3–4 × 4 Gy (lower dose due to prior dose).

## Target Volumes
- **HR-CTV (interstitial):** gross tumor + 1 cm margin.
- **GTV:** visible/palpable disease.
- **OARs (typically contoured):** urethra, anal canal, anterior rectum, bladder, femoral heads (if applicable).

## OAR Constraints (cumulative EQD2, α/β=3)
- **Urethra D0.1cc or D10:** ≤ 100–110 Gy EQD2.
- **Anal canal D2cc:** ≤ 65 Gy EQD2.
- **Rectum D2cc:** ≤ 65 Gy EQD2.
- **Bladder D2cc:** ≤ 80 Gy EQD2.
- **Skin (surface):** ≤ 60–70 Gy EQD2 to limit telangiectasia, fibrosis.

## Outcomes
- 5-year LC (primary): 60–80% (highly variable by stage).
- LC for recurrence: 50–70%.
- Late toxicity grade ≥3: 10–25% (skin, fibrosis, dyspareunia, urinary).

## Special Considerations
- **Multidisciplinary planning:** BT applicator insertion requires coordination with GYN oncology.
- **Imaging:** CT or MRI for delineation. Interstitial catheters visible on CT; MRI better for soft-tissue definition.
- **Dose rate:** HDR or PDR both used; PDR allows continuous outpatient treatment.
- **Anesthesia:** Interstitial cases often require spinal or general anesthesia.

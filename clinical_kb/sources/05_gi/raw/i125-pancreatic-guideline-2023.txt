Guidelines for permanent iodine-125 seed interstitial brachytherapy for pancreatic cancer (2023 edition): The Chinese expert consensus workshop report

ABSTRACT The incidence of pancreatic cancer is increasing worldwide. Approximately, 60% of patients with pancreatic cancer have distant metastases at the time of diagnosis, of which only 10% can be removed using standard resection. Further, patients derive limited benefits from chemotherapy or radiotherapy. As such, alternative methods to achieve local control have emerged, including permanent iodine125 seed interstitial brachytherapy. In 2023, the Chinese College of Interventionalists, affiliated with the Chinese Medical Doctor Association, organized a group of multidisciplinary experts to compose guidelines for this treatment modality. The aim of this conference was to standardize the procedure for permanent iodine125 seed interstitial brachytherapy, including indications, contraindications, preprocedural preparation, procedural operations, complications, efficacy evaluation, and followup.
KEY WORDS: Brachytherapy, guideline, iodine125, pancreatic cancer

Among all malignant tumors, pancreatic cancer ranks 14th in terms of incidence and seventh in mortality worldwide.[1] Between 2015 and 2019, the incidence of pancreatic cancer in the United States increased by approximately 1% annually in both men and women, with the lowest survival rate of all cancers.[2] Approximately 47.1% of new cases and 48.1% of deaths worldwide occur in Asia, followed in incidence by Europe and North America.[3] A recent analysis of 48 countries[4] showed that the incidence of pancreatic cancer has significantly increased in men and women in 14 and 17 countries, respectively. Furthermore, increased mortality was observed in men in eight countries and in women in 14 countries. Concurrently, the percentage of the national population older than 50 years increased in 18 countries.

Owing to the unbalanced development of medical and healthcare services in different regions of China, diagnosis and treatment protocols, including permanent iodine125 seed interstitial brachytherapy, need to be standardized. Therefore, in 2023, the Chinese College of Interventionalists, affiliated with the Chinese Medical Doctor Association, organized a group of multidisciplinary experts to compose a consensus through repeated discussions and consultations. This consensus is only applicable to malignant tumors of pancreatic ductal epithelial origin.
1. Concept and principles of permanent iodine125 seed interstitial brachytherapy.

2.2 Percutaneous transhepatic implantation of a biliary stent loaded with iodine125 seeds or iodine125 seed strands: This implantation method is primarily used for patients with pancreatic head cancer and obstructive jaundice. Biliary stents loaded with iodine125 seeds have a more uniform dose distribution and are suitable for patients with tumors surrounding the bile duct.[24] The iodine125 seed strand has an eccentric dose distribution and is suitable for tumors located on one side.[14]
2.3 Implantation during surgery: When a tumor is in close proximity to important structures, including the abdominal aorta, superior mesenteric artery, splenic artery, common bile duct, and duodenum, it cannot be radically removed. Under these conditions, iodine125 seeds can be implanted under US guidance.[25]

Iodine125 seeds with a size of 4.5 mm × 0.8 mm covered with an envelope of nickel titanium alloy are implanted directly into the lesion under image guidance. The halflife of iodine125 seeds is 59.6 days, and they can emit rays with an average energy of 35.5 KeV as well as Xrays with energies of 27.4 KeV and 31.4 KeV. This irradiation can inhibit cell proliferation and angiogenesis, induce apoptosis, and kill tumor cells.[20,21]
2. Implanting methods.
2.1 Imageguided percutaneous implantation: Imageguided modalities include computed tomography (CT)[22] and endoscopic ultrasound (EUS).[23] Due to its limited scanning range, EUS is commonly used for small lesions around the gastrointestinal tract. CT is considered the most commonly used guiding modality because of its high resolution and extensive detection. Therefore, CT was used as the guiding modality for this consensus.

3. Indications and contraindications.
3.1 Indications:  Tumors invade important surrounding structures and cannot be resected[22];  patients cannot tolerate tracheal intubation, general anesthesia, or surgical resection due to poor cardiopulmonary reserve;  patients refuse surgical resection[16];  disease progression after systemic therapy or other local treatments occurs[17];  iodine125 seeds could be implanted in the residual lesions or tumor beds when the tumor could not be radically removed during surgery.[25]
3.2 Relative contraindications:  concurrent distant metastasis: permanent iodine125 seed interstitial brachytherapy could be conducted to reduce tumor burden or alleviate pain[23];  obstructive jaundice: percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography drainage or biliary stent placement may be performed before the procedure, and permanent iodine125

4. Preprocedural preparation.

4.1 Multidisciplinary treatment.

Multidisciplinary treatment (MDT) consultation with physicians in the Department of Pancreatic Surgery, Gastroenterology, Oncology, Radiation Oncology, and Interventional Medicine was recommended to ensure that the patients received standardized treatment [Figure 1].

4.2 Preprocedural evaluation and treatment planning system (TPS): The evaluation and TPS plan rely on a procedure based on standard pancreatic CT images. The scanning protocol parameters were as follows: multipleslice spiral CT (MSCT), slice thickness (0.5 mm), and an injection rate of 3–5 mL/s for iodine contrast agent. Images of the pancreatic parenchymal phase were obtained 40–50s following contrast agent injection, and images of the portal vein phase were obtained 65–70s after injection.[26] MSCT was used to demonstrate the tumor size, location, and relationship with adjacent organs or tissues (including the gastrointestinal tract, biliary tract, and blood vessels). Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or positron emission tomography (PET)–CT are recommended to detect or rule out distant metastasis.

The activity of iodine125 seeds should be set to 0.3–0.8 mci. The planning target volume (PTV) should cover one more centimeter beyond the margin of the gross tumor volume (GTV). The prescribed dose was 110–130 Gy.[8]

The CT images were imported into the TPS, and the PTV and organs at risk (OAR) near the tumor were delineated. The number of iodine125 seeds was calculated using the TPS to ensure the prescribed dose. A dose–volume histogram (DVH) is used for dose evaluation.

4.3 Standard lab panel: complete blood count, coagulation function, liver and kidney function, blood glucose, CA199, electrocardiogram, and pulmonary function tests.

4.4 Patient preparation: The patient should be maintained on a liquid diet for 18 hours preceding the procedure and take a laxative (such as polyethylene glycol electrolyte powder) 15 hours before the procedure to promote bowel movement. Fasting should be adhered to for 12 h before the procedure, and a nasogastric tube should be placed. Somatostatin should be intravenous injected continuously at a rate of 0.25 mg/h

4.5 Pathological diagnosis: If possible, an EUSguided fineneedle biopsy (FNB)[27] or percutaneous fineneedle aspiration biopsy (FNAB)[28] should be performed before the procedure to confirm the diagnosis.
5. Procedural operation.
5.1 Position fixation and anesthesia: The position of the patient should be selected according to the tumor location and patient's condition. The supine position is most commonly used. Intravenous general anesthesia and moderate sedation with a 0.1mg fentanyl bolus and dexmedetomidine infusion at a rate of 1.5 g/kg/h are recommended.
5.2 Iodine125 seed implantation: CT should be performed with a slice thickness of 5 mm to demonstrate tumor location and size. The puncture plane (intercostal space) should be selected based on tumor location, size, and preprocedural TPS plan. The blood and bile ducts must be avoided. Iodine125 seeds should be implanted using an 18gauge puncture needle according to the TPS plan.[8] Fluorouracil with a 0.05ml bolus for one seed should be injected through the puncture needle to prevent tumor seeding. Upon completion of the procedure, abdominal CT should be performed to verify the distribution of the iodine125 seeds. The dosage should be verified to confirm whether it matches the preprocedural TPS plan.
5.3 Intraprocedural monitoring: Vital signs such as heart rate, blood pressure, and blood oxygen saturation should be closely monitored during the procedure. Patient consciousness, abdominal pain, nausea, dyspnea, or difficulty in breathing should be noted.
5.4 Postprocedural management: The implanted area was covered with a leadprotective pad with a halfvalue layer of 0.25 mm. Continuous electrocardiographic monitoring and oxygen supplementation should be provided until the patient's condition stabilized. After the patient's bowel movements have recovered, the gastric tube should be removed. Somatostatin and antibiotics must then be continued for 72 hours after the procedure. Complete blood count, coagulation function, liver and kidney function, blood glucose, CA199, blood, and urine amylase levels should be checked on the third day after the procedure. The clinical manifestations of pancreatitis should be closely monitored.
6. Complications and treatment.
6.1 Bleeding: When the tumor surrounds the celiac trunk, hepatic artery, superior mesenteric artery, or vein, the puncture needle may damage blood vessels.[29] If bleeding occurs, the needle core should be reinserted, slowly withdrawn (0.5 cm), and held for approximately 1 min before implanting 1–2 seeds. In most cases, bleeding can be stopped using this method; however, if the bleeding volume is large (>500 ml), blood transfusion is recommended. In such cases, arterial

angiography can be performed to identify and embolize the responsible blood vessels.
6.2 Pancreatitis and pancreatic fistula: These are rare but serious complications usually caused by damage to the pancreatic duct. If the patient experiences severe abdominal pain after the procedure, with signs of peritoneal irritation and elevated blood and urine amylase levels, abdominal CT is recommended to validate the diagnosis of pancreatitis and pancreatic fistulas. Drainage should be performed, and drugs that suppress pancreatic enzyme secretion should be used to relieve symptoms.
6.3 Iodine125 seed displacement: Seeds may move after the procedure; migrate to the liver, spleen, and stomach; or become detached and free in the abdominal cavity.[29] A single seed causes very little damage to the surrounding tissue but should still be closely observed.
7. Combined therapy.
Pancreatic cancer is a malignant tumor that benefits from combination therapy, including intraarterial infusion chemotherapy combined with permanent iodine125 seed interstitial brachytherapy,[30,31] chemotherapy before and after local treatment/surgical resection,[32] poly (ADPribose) polymerase inhibitors,[33] and programmed death receptor1 inhibitors.[34] Li et al.[21] showed that gemcitabine plays a synergistic role in the apoptosis and cell cycle arrest of PANC1 cells induced by iodine125 seeds through increases in the Bax/Bcl2 ratio. Based on the results of basic research and clinical practice, Yuliang Li proposed the concept of regional concurrent chemoradiotherapy (RCCR), that is, permanent iodine-125 seed interstitial brachytherapy at first, and then trans-arterial infusion chemotherapy. Chemotherapeutic drugs (such as gemcitabine) concentrated in the tumor area are expected to enhance the killing effect of iodine125 seeds on pancreatic cancer cells, thereby representing a new comprehensive interventional treatment for pancreatic cancer.
8. Efficacy evaluation and followup.
8.1 Postoperative efficacy evaluation: Efficacy evaluation criteria version 1.1 for solid tumors.[35] Complete relief (CR): All target lesions disappear, no new lesions appear, and tumor markers are normal for at least 4 weeks. Partial relief (PR): The sum of the maximum diameters of the target lesions decreases by 30%, lasting for at least 4 weeks. Progressive disease (PD): The sum of the maximum diameters of the target lesions increases by 20%, or new lesions appear. Stable disease (SD): The degree of reduction in target lesions does not reach the level of PR, and the degree of increase does not reach the level of PD but falls between the two.
